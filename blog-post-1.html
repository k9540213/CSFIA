Can You Trust What You Read Online? A CRAAP Test Showdown

Posted on June 23, 2025

This week in my Information Literacy class, we're getting into the most important skill of all: figuring out if what you're reading online is actually reliable. It's easy to find information, but how do you know if it's good information?

The tool my professor wants us to use is called the CRAAP test. And despite the funny name, it’s a surprisingly solid framework. It’s an acronym that stands for Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose.

To put it to the test, I took two websites from a previous search on the cognitive benefits of bilingualism and put them head-to-head.

"The CRAAP test gives you a simple but effective checklist to make sure you're not citing a slick advertisement in the middle of your research paper."

The first site was from the Dana Foundation, a non-profit focused on brain research. The second was from a commercial translation company. While the company's article was more recent, the Dana Foundation's article was written by Ph.D.s and cited actual research, making it far more authoritative and accurate. The purpose was clear: one was to inform, the other was to sell. It was a perfect illustration of why you always have to ask 'why' a piece of information exists.
